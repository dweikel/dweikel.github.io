The Brown Box

Concepted in 1967

Your journey starts here with a mysterious brown box. Developed by Ralph Baer in 1967, it was the first prototype of a video game console. The name Brown Box comes from the brown tape Baer and his team wrapped the device in, which mimicked wood veneer. It was primitive, but this little box would pave the way for an entire industry.

Read more about the Brown Box!

Magnavox Odyssey

Released in 1972
350k units sold

Magnavox was the company who bought and invested in Ralph Baer's invention. The curious little device would later be developed into the Odyssey. It was simply a sophisticated Pong machine, requiring two players who could engage in games like “Cat and Mouse,” “Ski Mountain,” and “Simon Says.” The simulated graphics were restricted to three dots, and a vertical line on the screen. Colors and visuals were implemented with the use of thin plastic screen overlays.

Read more about Odyssey!

Pong Consoles

Released through 1972—1977

“Pong” is widely considered the first video game. While this isn't technically true since the Odyssey had the first set of video games, it was the first game to inspire companies to start developing video games. The first “Pong” game was developed by Allan Alcorn, and the first commerical release made by Atari. Originally meant to played in a cabinet, many companies started creating their own home versions of pong. Companies like Sears, Coleco, and even Nintendo developed their own versions of Pong. There were plenty of lawsuits fought over who had the rights to “Pong.”

Read more about Pong Consoles, there's a ton of them!

Atari 2600

Released in 1977
30 million units sold

The Atari 2600 (Twenty-six-hundred) was the console which really kick-started the entire home console industry. Arcades were popular at the time, and this system allowed arcade goers to bring a video gaming experience into their living rooms, but also allowed them to play different games via cartridges. Game difficulty and modifications could be applied by switches on the console. Adjusted to inflation, this thing would cost over $780!

Read more about 2600!

Arcades

Most popular through 1978—1982

While arcade cabinets are not techinally home consoles, their influence on the market and game design was imperative in the late 70's and 80's. In 1981, the arcade industry was worth little over 8 billion dollars, which is equal to about 21 billion today. Many prominent games started in an arcades, classics like Pac-Man, Galaga, Space Invaders, and many more! These games would eventually be licensed to Atari for development on the Atari 2600.

Read more about the Golden Age of Arcades!

Tiger Electronic Handheld Games

Released throughout the '90s

Tiger Electronics was a subsidary business of Hasbro, and was responsible for flooding the market with cheap, plastic handheld games. Trying to capitalize off the success of the Gameboy, Tiger released individual games in their custom casing. Each Tiger game was it's own game, there was no need for cartridges. However, these games were produced inexpensively, and it really shows. the graphics are all static image cells, comparable to digital watches. The controls are also very primitive, with some only having a forward and shoot button. The Tiger Game.com was surprisingly innovative, as it was the first handheld game device with internet access and a touchscreen.

Read more about Tiger Electronics!

Mattell Microvision

Released in 1979

This special little device is the very first handheld console ever produced! The Microvision unfortunately had a lot of technical blunders, and were primitively assmebled. The screens were prone to rotting, the microprocessor was easily damaged, and the 12 button keyboard did not age well. The console sold surprisingly well by most reports, (none of which have a definite number) but due to lack of support and only 13 released games for the console, it was discontinued two years after it's release.

Read more about Microvision!

Vectrex

Released in 1982

The Vectrex is the first, and only console to ever be based entirely with vector graphics. The console also included it's own screen, which looks like a little tube television. The system would play games on cartridges, and since it could not simulate color, games would come packaged with a thin plastic screen overlay specifically made for the game.

Read more about Vectrex!

Turbographyx16

Released in 1987
5.8 million units sold

Pronounced: “Turbo Graphics Sixteen”
Hudson Soft and NEC joined forces to create the first ever 16-bit console. Unlike other consoles of it's time which played games on cartridges, the TurboGraphyx16 played games on data cards known as HuCards. The cards were designed by Hudson Soft, who were trying to pitch their advanced cards to Nintendo at the time. When Nintendo refused, Hudson Soft would not back down and found another investor. This console once held the world record for smallest console!

Neo Geo

Released in 1991
980k units sold

Neo Geo was the closest a consumer could get to having an actual arcade game in their home without the cabinet. The console initially sold for $650, which is about $1000 today. While the console did not sell well due to it's immensely high price tag, the console has a cult following even to this day, with both commercial and personal bootlegging. Neo Geo was the first console to introduce memory cards, allowing players to save their game progress on an easily transportable card.

The Video Game Market Crash

Released in 1983

After the console market was flooded with numerous Pong consoles and Atari 2600 copycats, and inferior quality games such as Pac-Man and the infamous E.T. (which was single handedly developed by Howard Scott Warshaw in only five weeks, talk about pressure!) for the 2600, the video game market took a massive hit. The entire industry dropped by nearly 97% and dissolved Atari into several companies, which were sold to the highest bidders. Atari buried a large amount of defunct consoles and game cartridges in the Alamogordo Desert, dubbed as the Atari Landfill.

Atari 5200 and Atari 7800

The 5200 released in 1982
1 million units sold,

The 7800 released in 1986
100k units sold

The Atari 5200 (Fifty-two-hundred) was Atari's attempt to captalize on the 2600's success, but it could not compare. It was released to little fanfare, as it was very bulky system, was not compatible with the expasnive library of 2600 games, and were prone to malfunctions, specifically with the controller.
To address the poor functionality of the 5200, Atari released the 7800 which sought to fix the issues with their previous console. It was too little too late unfortunately, because the system tanked. By the end of the 1986, there were only 10 games for the console.

Atari Lynx

Released in 1989
3 million units sold

The Atari Lynx marks when Atari would start naming their consoles after cats instead of numbers. It was first handheld console with a color LCD screen. Atari was trying to use superior technology to gain a better foothold in the market, but without support from game developers, it took third place behind Nintendo and Sega.

Read more about Lynx!

Atari Jaguar

Released in 1993
under 250k units sold

Atari would release one final console in the early 90's to try and regain their once dominant position in the gaming market. Their strategy was yet again to blow away the competition in hardware specifications. The Atari Jaguar was the first ever 64-bit console, and was released to overhwlming negativity and criticism. Because of the complex nature of the processors added together to create the 64-bits the console boasted, developers found it very difficult to make games on the console, and often games ended up looking no better than games on other “inferior” systems. The Jaguar struck out with a lackluster library of games, and was plagued by technical issues. These failures drove Atari out of console development permanently.

and Nintendo Gameboy

61 million units sold,

Gameboy released in 1989
118 million units sold

Known as the Famicom in Japan, Nintendo of America released the NES in 1985, and it completely turned the failing gaming market around, and back into a prosperous industry again. One in four American households had a NES in the 1980's, and it's smash hit release title, Super Mario Bros. would redefine game design to come. The Gameboy was released in 1989, and allowed players to bring their games with them wherever they went, as long as they had some batteries. The Gameboy and it's variants reigned supreme over any competing handheld system, which Nintendo has continued to do with their handhelds.

Nintendo's successor to the popular NES, the SNES was released as a competitor to the Sega Genesis, which had tipped the balance of power Nintendo had on the industry for so long. The system launched with Super Mario World, which was by far the best selling game for the console with 20 million copies sold worldwide.

Nintendo Virtual Boy and Tiger R-Zone

Virtual Boy released in 1995
770k units sold,

R-Zone released in 1995
??? units sold

Nintendo sought to bridge the gap between the SNES and the release of the N64. They were working on a virtual reality concept, and decided to push it forward. Originally dubbed VR32, the Virtual Boy was the first virtual reality console, but failed miserably. The “portable” console could only render games in red and black, and were prone to giving users migraines within several minutes. It's essentially a pair of binoculaurs on a stand, which as James Rolfe who portrays The Angry Video Game Nerd stated: “That equates to a pair of eyeballs on legs.” There were also only 22 games developed for the Virtual Boy, and was discontinued before Nintendo could properly support the platform.
Tiger Electronics was always trying to compete with Nintendo, especially with their inexpensive handheld games. After seeing the Virtual Boy displayed at conferences, Tiger developed their own version, called the R–Zone. It had a headstrap, and used reflective plastic to display the typical “digital watch graphics” in red and black. The view for the player was situated only over the right eye, meaning player's would have to strain their left eye to see the game properly. It also only had 21 games made for it, which makes it worse than the Virtual Boy in every aspect. Well, at least they added a head-strap.

Master System released in 1986
16 million units sold,
Genesis released in 1989

Sega would emerge with the Master System in late 1985, as a direct competitor to Nintendo. However, Sega struggled to gain a foothold in the market which was dominantly owned by Nintendo (80%) and Atari (12%) at the time. Sega would later find success in the market with the release of their bold, 16-bit console Genesis.
The Genesis really highlighted the competition between Sega and Nintendo, with it's edgy and hip marketing. The console's slogan proudly boasted: “Genesis does what Nintendon't!” Sega found success due their advanced hardware and “blast processing” which allowed for faster-paced gameplay, improved sound and music quality, and better graphics. Sonic the Hedgehog would become Sega's mascot when the title was released in 1991.

Sega Dreamcast

Released in 1999
9 million units sold

Sega's final contender in the console market was the Dreamcast. The name comes from combining the words “dream” and “broadcast.” The Dreamcast's fatal flaw is how progressive it was. The Dreamcast's hardware was far ahead of it's time, and sold poorly only due to the anticipation of the PS2. It was the first home console to include online play out of the box, as long as you had a dial-up connection. The SegaNet, the main platform for online gaming and downloads for the Dreamcast, laid the foundations for online console gaming hitting the mainstream in the mid 2000's. The Dreamcast has a devout cult following, and like the Neo Geo, has a commercial and personal bootlegging community.

Welcome to Hub World!

A Hub World is described as the zone in video games where one can choose sets of levels, individual levels and challenges. This map serves as a Hub World, cataloging almost every video game console released in North America. Video gaming has become such an important and prevalent phenomenon around the world, and is rapidly progressing forward gathering the attention of more and more people. It's so easy to get caught up with the latest and greatest in technology, we forget the relics left behind and their impact on our culture. This is especially true in the world of video gaming.
This map serves an ode to the rich history of video game consoles in North America. So tread onward dauntlessly, and discover what Hub World has to offer!
